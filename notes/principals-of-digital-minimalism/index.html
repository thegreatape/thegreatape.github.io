Principals of Digital Minimalism

  1. Clutter is costly: The costs of having your attention pulled by too many apps and services overwhelms the benefits.
  2. Optimization is important: To get the most of out of the technology you chose to use, it’s critical to be carefully strategic in how and when you chose to employ it.
    • Interesting observation about the law of diminishing returns: a small amount of time carefully invested in a particular technology is actually more likely to bring the most benefit for the least time invested.
  3. Intentionality is satisfying: Being intentional in one’s use of tech is satisfying in and of itself.

I think I am finding the ideas here compelling without necessarily finding Newport’s arguments for them convincing.

Declutter (started 2/10/19)

  • Set aside 30 days to take a break from optional, non-critical technologies
  • Reconnect with satisfying / meaningful activities during that time period
  • Afterwards, slowly reintroduce tech you find:
    • Still supports things that you deeply value
    • Is the best way to use technology to further this value
    • With a plan for how to optimize value for time invested—the “how & when” to use it.
  • Plan beforehand what you will replace the time you’re currently spending on optional tech
  • Use operating procedures when there’s an optional tech with a few critical-path uses—that is, specify how and when exactly you will use said tech

Interesting Practice Ideas

  • Long walks (yeah, already doing with the doggo)
  • Writing letters to yourself (journaling?)
  • Use tech to facilitate in-person interaction (instead of replacing it)
    • Consolidate the use of these tools (e.g. texting) to set times during the day. For example, only check your text messages at a few scheduled times during the day.
  • Make leisure plans:
    • Seasonal, goal-oriented ones
    • Weekly schedules blocking out time and listing habits
  • Join in-person groups
  • For low-quality leisure time: schedule and timeblock it (ex. you can surf / check feeds for 30 minutes after lunch on Saturday)
  • Build or fix something every week, ideally with your hands
  • Make your computers (mobile and desktop) able to become single-purpose devices for whatever purpose you need at the time
  • Embrace “slow media”: find the best, high-quality source of info, pare them down to the minimum needed and consume them on a schedule.
  • Use social media “like a professional”: figure out what you need, get it with as little time / interaction as you can.
